### Decisions made during session:
- **SOCO:** Revised to "They've already figured out what I'm stuck on."
- **Hero:** No subhead. Headline stands alone + simple low-friction CTA button. Sugarman chain: each line compels the next.
- **"Put Animalz in the loop":** Better as section header than CTA button — it introduces a concept, which confuses as a button.
- **Page structure reordered (v2):** Hero → Silver bullet (reader's world) → What we learned (credibility) → Stakes → Workflows/proof → CTA. Silver bullet creates the question that "what we learned" answers.
- **Sullivan's principle:** Don't explain the headline's implication. Reader gets it. Show expertise, don't claim it.
- **Singular "system":** "We built our own system" not "systems." More concrete.

### Working copy for Section 2 (What We Bring):
> We're content people who had to become AI builders. All the tools we tried delivered copycat content, so we went through workflow hell and back to build our own system.
> [Then straight into positioning pillars — what the system looks like / what we bring]

### Parked idea: Silver Bullet Section (for Section 3 — DIY Trap)
**Concept:** People think buying the tool solves the problem. Like buying a productivity book and feeling like the problem is solved — but you haven't read it yet. 80% fail at the actual application.

Same dynamic with AirOps: "We bought AirOps, problem solved." But actually, that's just the starting line. Behind mountains are more mountains. Maps to the AI Onion — data infrastructure, experience layer, integrations, org change. Each is another mountain you didn't see when you bought the tool.

**Frame:** "You bought the tool. That was the easy part."

**Why it works here:** Validates the reader's experience without blaming them. Reframes the problem as structural (layers of complexity) not personal (your team failed). Naturally leads into why you need a partner who's already climbed those mountains.
