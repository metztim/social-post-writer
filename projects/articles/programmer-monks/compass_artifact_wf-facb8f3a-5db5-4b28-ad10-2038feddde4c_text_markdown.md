# From scribes to software: the literacy analogy for our AI moment

**AI coding tools likely represent a "printing press moment" for software creation — but one that is categorically different and far faster than the original.** The analogy between the five-thousand-year journey from ancient scribes to mass literacy and the eighty-year arc from ENIAC to vibe coding is genuinely illuminating: both trace a path from tiny priesthoods of specialists guarding arcane symbol systems through progressive democratization toward universal access. Yet the analogy has a critical flaw. The printing press reproduced text but never composed it. AI generates code, making it simultaneously tool *and* practitioner — a dynamic without historical precedent. Understanding where the analogy holds and where it fractures is essential for anticipating what comes next.

## Five millennia of literacy in fast-forward

The arc of writing follows a remarkably consistent pattern: a tiny elite guards the technology, social forces gradually widen access, a breakthrough technology accelerates distribution, and centuries of institutional change eventually deliver mass participation.

**Egyptian scribes** comprised roughly **1% of the population**, trained from boyhood in schools where "a boy's ear is on his back: he hears when he is beaten." They enjoyed immunity from manual labor and controlled taxation, treasury, and public works. Mesopotamian cuneiform scribes were similarly rare — perhaps one in several hundred urbanites — constrained by the sheer complexity of hundreds of signs. Classical Athens reached an estimated **5–10% literacy**, boosted by the Greek alphabet's simplicity and democracy's incentives to read posted laws. The Roman Empire peaked at perhaps **10–15% overall**, with **20–30%** in cities like Pompeii, where abundant graffiti testifies to literate soldiers, merchants, and freedmen. Marcus Aurelius wrote his *Meditations* in Greek, epitomizing an elite expected to be thoroughly bilingual — but the vast agricultural majority remained untouched.

When Rome collapsed, literacy retreated into monasteries. Benedict of Nursia's Monte Cassino (529 AD) and Cassiodorus's Vivarium made manuscript copying a sacred duty. **A single Bible could take one monk 20 years** and require the skins of 170 calves. Scriptoria accommodated perhaps a dozen monks at a time, working six hours daily, leaving marginal pleas of exhaustion in their manuscripts. European literacy fell to perhaps **1–2%**, confined almost entirely to clergy.

The critical and often overlooked period between monks and Gutenberg — roughly 1200 to 1450 — saw a series of compounding innovations. The University of Bologna (1088), Paris, and Oxford created demand for secular academic texts that monasteries couldn't supply, generating a new ecosystem of stationers, scribes, and the *pecia* system for lending quires to students. **Paper arrived in Europe** through Islamic intermediaries (Xàtiva, Spain, by the twelfth century; Fabriano, Italy, 1276), costing roughly **one-sixth the price of parchment** and enabling far greater output. Vernacular literature emerged — Dante's *Divine Comedy* (c. 1308), Chaucer's *Canterbury Tales* (1390s) — making reading relevant beyond Latin-educated clergy. A literate merchant class grew as commerce demanded contracts, account books, and correspondence. By the time Gutenberg assembled his press around 1440, the infrastructure for a reading revolution was already in place.

## The printing press was necessary but spectacularly insufficient

Gutenberg's innovation — combining movable metal type, oil-based ink, and the screw press — produced **3,600 pages per workday** versus a few by hand. By 1500, presses operated in over 200 cities across 12 European countries, and somewhere between **9 and 20 million volumes** had been printed. Luther's 95 Theses (1517) spread across Germany in two weeks and across Europe in two months; roughly **300,000 copies** of his works circulated within three years. The Reformation, fueled by cheap vernacular Bibles, drove school-building across Protestant northern Europe, creating a feedback loop: more literacy → more readers → more demand → more printing → more literacy.

Yet **mass literacy took roughly 400 additional years** after Gutenberg. England progressed from about 10% literacy in the late 1400s to 30% by the 1640s, 60% by 1800, and finally 97% by 1900. France lagged further. Portugal remained majority-illiterate into the 1940s. The intervening centuries required pamphlets, newspapers, the novel, coffeehouses, scientific academies, the Enlightenment, and ultimately compulsory education laws — Prussia in 1763, England in 1880, France in 1882. The printing press lit the fuse; social, economic, religious, and political institutions had to carry the flame across four centuries.

The causal chain was unmistakable: printing press → cheaper books → Reformation → more schools → more literacy → Scientific Revolution → Enlightenment → democratic revolutions. Paine's *Common Sense* (1776) sold 500,000 copies. Diderot's *Encyclopédie* (1751–1772) put **25,000 copies** of comprehensive knowledge into circulation before the French Revolution. Each stage built on the previous one, but none was instantaneous.

## The computing parallel maps with surprising precision

The stages of computing history align almost uncannily with the literacy arc — until they don't.

**The "ancient scribes" era (1940s–1950s)** mirrors the earliest writing civilizations. ENIAC's six original programmers were women mathematicians recruited from a pool of 200 "human computers," programming by physically rewiring cables — a process that took weeks on paper and days to configure. Fewer than **a dozen operational computers** existed worldwide by 1950. The global "literacy rate" for computing was effectively **less than 0.001%** — comparable to cuneiform scribes in Mesopotamia. Programs were written in raw binary, and the social status of programming was low, considered clerical work.

**The "monastic priesthood" era (1960s–1980s)** produced the computing equivalent of scriptoria. Mainframe operators, often literally wearing **white lab coats**, mediated all access to machines. Users submitted punch cards and waited hours or days for printouts — never touching the computer directly. A 1976 BYTE magazine article explicitly referenced the "Software Priesthood." FORTRAN (1957), COBOL (1959), and C (1972) were the liturgical languages of this era. The United States had roughly **10,000 programmers in 1967**, growing tenfold to **100,000 by 1977**. Margaret Hamilton coined "software engineering" during the Apollo program, fighting to give the profession legitimacy. Like medieval monks, these programmers guarded arcane knowledge essential to civilization's infrastructure — but incomprehensible to outsiders.

**The "university" moment (1980s–1990s)** parallels the medieval founding of Bologna and Oxford. Personal computers — the Apple II, IBM PC, Commodore 64 — arrived with BASIC in ROM, enabling anyone who turned on the machine to write programs immediately. In the UK, the BBC Micro sold **1.5 million units** and introduced a generation to coding. CS enrollments exploded; by 1981, **9% of American high school graduates wanted to study computer science**. A hacker culture crystallized around the Homebrew Computer Club, Dr. Dobb's Journal, and a spirit of antiestablishment sharing — the intellectual ferment of a new university system.

**The "arrival of paper" (1990s–2000s)** maps to the internet era. Just as paper reduced the cost of writing material sixfold, the web reduced the cost of distributing and learning software to essentially zero. Tim Berners-Lee's HTML had just **18 elements** — anyone could "View Source," learn, and create. GeoCities hosted millions of personal websites. Stack Overflow (2008) became the universal reference library. The open-source movement (Linux, the LAMP stack, GitHub) created a free infrastructure for building anything. The global developer population surged past **10 million** by the 2000s. Like paper's arrival in Europe, the internet didn't cause the revolution directly — it removed a critical bottleneck.

## Are AI coding tools the Gutenberg press?

The case is strong. GitHub Copilot now has **20 million users** and generates **46% of all code** written by active users (61% in Java). **Ninety percent of Fortune 100 companies** have adopted it. Andrej Karpathy coined **"vibe coding"** in February 2025 — "fully give in to the vibes, embrace exponentials, and forget that the code even exists" — and Collins Dictionary named it Word of the Year for 2025. Twenty-five percent of Y Combinator's Winter 2025 batch had codebases that were **95% AI-generated**. The AI coding tools market reached **$7.37 billion in 2025**, and Gartner forecasts that 90% of enterprise software engineers will use AI assistants by 2028.

Karpathy frames this as **"Software 3.0"**: Software 1.0 was hand-coded programs, Software 2.0 was neural network weights, and Software 3.0 uses natural language as the programming interface. His assessment is that we're still in the **"1960s era"** of this revolution — the equivalent of mainframes and time-sharing, with the full PC revolution still ahead. Jensen Huang declared at the 2024 World Government Summit: "Everybody in the world is now a programmer." Satya Nadella predicts AI will create **one billion developers**, drawing an explicit parallel to how word processors made a billion people typists.

The low-code/no-code movement reinforces this trajectory. The market reached **$45.5 billion in 2025**. Gartner predicts **citizen developers will outnumber professional developers 4:1** at large enterprises, and 70% of new enterprise applications will use low-code technologies by 2026. Already, **60% of custom apps** are built outside IT departments.

## If the analogy holds, here is what comes next

The post-printing-press sequence — pamphlets, newspapers, novels, Reformation, Scientific Revolution, Enlightenment, democratic revolutions — suggests a roadmap.

**Pamphlets are already here.** Vibe-coded quick apps, personal automation scripts, and micro-software created by non-programmers in hours mirror the cheap, disposable pamphlets of the 1500s. Scott H. Young describes making custom helper applications that "would have been prohibitively expensive to create" — the computational equivalent of a political broadsheet.

**The "Reformation" — the disruption of tech gatekeepers — is underway.** Small teams now achieve output previously reserved for large engineering departments. Andrew Chen predicts "most code will be written by the time-rich... kids and students rather than software engineers." The traditional career path of the junior developer faces existential threat, just as scribes attacked Parisian printing presses in 1476.

**The "Scientific Revolution" equivalent** would be new forms of knowledge creation enabled by universal software fluency. Karpathy envisions AI agents that specialize, collaborate, and create emergent capabilities — computational systems that generate insight, not merely process information. GitHub research suggests AI developer productivity could boost global GDP by **over $1.5 trillion**.

**"Mass literacy" — universal programming ability** — remains the distant horizon. If the historical pattern holds, the printing press moment of the 2020s might not produce true mass software literacy until the 2060s or later, even accounting for the compressed timescale of technology. The literacy analogy suggests that tools alone don't suffice; you need institutions (educational reform), cultural shifts (the equivalent of the Protestant emphasis on personal Scripture reading), and economic imperatives (the Industrial Revolution making literacy essential for workers).

## Five critical ways the analogy fractures

Despite its power, the literacy parallel breaks down in important and instructive ways.

**First, AI is both printing press and author.** The Gutenberg press reproduced text but never composed a sentence. AI generates functional code from natural language descriptions. This collapses the distinction between tool and practitioner. When Copilot writes 46% of code, the question shifts from "who can write programs?" to "who can evaluate and direct programs?" — a fundamentally different kind of literacy closer to Chris Granger's concept of "modeling" than to traditional coding skill.

**Second, the timescale is radically compressed.** Writing evolved over five millennia; computing has gone from ENIAC to vibe coding in eighty years. The printing press took four centuries to produce mass literacy. AI coding tools are being adopted by millions within months. This compression means that the social institutions — education systems, professional norms, regulatory frameworks — cannot adapt at the pace of the technology, creating turbulence that the gradual literary revolution mostly avoided.

**Third, code executes while text is inert.** A misplaced comma rarely prevents comprehension of a sentence; a misplaced semicolon crashes a program. More importantly, code takes action in the world — moving money, controlling machines, making decisions. The security implications are starkly real: **45% of AI-generated code contains security flaws**, and one analysis found a **tenfold increase** in monthly security findings at Fortune 50 enterprises between December 2024 and June 2025. The "mass literacy" of code carries safety risks that mass textual literacy never posed.

**Fourth, abstraction makes programming potentially invisible rather than universal.** To read, you must engage with letters — there's no "graphical user interface" for text. But billions use software daily without any programming knowledge. Computing's deepening abstraction stack (transistors → machine code → high-level languages → GUIs → natural language interfaces) may make programming increasingly *hidden* rather than increasingly *widespread*. Rushkoff frames this as precisely the danger — people who don't understand code are "programmed" by it — but it challenges the core analogy: we don't call everyone "literate in papermaking" just because they read books.

**Fifth, writing encodes natural language, which every human naturally acquires; programming encodes formal logic, which requires deliberate training.** The cognitive substrate is different. Language acquisition is biologically prepared; programming skill is not. This raises a genuine question about whether universal programming fluency is achievable — or even desirable — in the way that universal reading fluency was.

## A rich intellectual tradition has explored this territory

The analogy has deep roots. **Seymour Papert** effectively launched the conversation in 1980 with *Mindstorms*, arguing that children could use computers as "objects-to-think-with" and coining the term "computational thinking." His student **Mitchel Resnick** created Scratch and reframed the goal as "computational fluency," explicitly comparing coding to writing: "Most people won't grow up to become professional programmers, but developing fluency with coding is valuable for everyone." **Marc Prensky** popularized the phrase "programming is the new literacy" in a 2008 Edutopia essay, predicting programming would leave "the back room" to become a marker of intellectual class.

The most rigorous academic treatment came from **Annette Vee** in *Coding Literacy* (MIT Press, 2017), which traced how both writing and coding were "institutionalized, domesticated, and then established as a basis for literacy," arguing that a "computational mentality" is emerging even where coding skill is not universal. **Jeannette Wing's** influential 2006 paper on "Computational Thinking" explicitly invoked the printing press: "Just as the printing press facilitated the spread of the three Rs, computing and computers will enable the spread of computational thinking." **Douglas Rushkoff's** *Program or Be Programmed* (2010, updated 2024) framed each new communication technology as creating a literacy divide between creators and consumers.

The most provocative counterpoint came from **Chris Granger** in his 2015 essay "Coding Is Not the New Literacy," which argued the real skill isn't coding but **modeling** — "creating a representation of a system that can be explored or used." He pointed to Excel as the true democratization tool, used by millions to model aspects of their lives without writing a line of traditional code. **Bret Victor's** work pushed further, arguing computing requires entirely new cognitive tools — new *media for thinking* — rather than simply wider access to existing programming languages. **Alan Kay** had perhaps the grandest vision: that computer literacy for children would produce adults with "new forms of critical thought, similar to the difference that literacy brought to society."

## Conclusion: a rhyme, not a repetition

The most honest assessment is that history rhymes but doesn't repeat. The literacy analogy illuminates real structural parallels — a priestly class guarding powerful symbol manipulation, progressive democratization through institutional and technological change, and transformative social consequences when access widens. The current AI moment genuinely resembles the printing press era: a sudden, dramatic reduction in the cost and skill required to produce the fundamental artifact (books then, software now).

But three features make this moment genuinely novel. The compressed timescale means social adaptation cannot keep pace — we're experiencing centuries of literary-equivalent change in decades, generating the simultaneous euphoria and anxiety visible in the data (developer trust in AI tools dropped from **43% to 29%** even as usage rose to 84%). The AI-as-author problem means the end state may not be "everyone programs" but rather "everyone directs AI that programs" — a literacy more akin to management than to writing. And the technical debt evidence — code duplication up **fourfold**, refactoring down from 25% to under 10% of changed lines — suggests that the "pamphlet era" of vibe coding may produce the same flood of low-quality material that early printing did, requiring decades to develop quality norms, critical evaluation skills, and institutional filters.

The deepest insight from the analogy may be temporal. If it took **400 years** after Gutenberg to achieve mass literacy — even compressed tenfold by modern institutional speed — the full social transformation of democratized software creation is a **multi-decade project**, not a product launch. The printing press was 1450; the Enlightenment was 1750. The tools are arriving now. The revolution they enable has barely begun.
