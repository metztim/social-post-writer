I've been tracking AI bubble commentary in my reading for the past five months, and the narrative arc is wild. Back in October, the smart money was nearly unanimous: yes, it's a bubble. M.G. Siegler [called it a bet that could bust](https://spyglass.org/ai-bubble/). Ben Thompson [wrote that bubbles build infrastructure](https://stratechery.com/2025/the-benefits-of-bubbles/) -- framing it as painful but productive. Howard Marks published a [careful Oaktree memo](https://www.oaktreecapital.com/insights/memo/is-it-a-bubble) drawing dotcom parallels. Cal Newport wrote in the New Yorker that [AI agents failed to live up to their hype](https://calnewport.com/why-didnt-ai-join-the-workforce-in-2025/). Even Derek Thompson -- who'd flirted with the counter-case in his October piece ["Why AI Is Not a Bubble"](https://www.derekthompson.org/p/why-ai-is-not-a-bubble) -- just had Paul Kedrosky back on to declare ["Yes, AI is a bubble. There is no question."](https://www.derekthompson.org/p/yes-ai-is-a-bubble-there-is-no-question)

Then something shifted. Dario Amodei wrote about models solving unsolved math. Matt Shumer -- an AI founder, not a pundit -- published ["Something Big Is Happening"](https://shumer.dev/something-big-is-happening) and said the "hitting a wall" debate is over. Nvidia launched the Rubin platform at CES with 10x cost reductions. Enterprise ROI data started showing up -- Deloitte's latest report says 88% of organizations report AI-driven revenue impact. And this month, Ben Thompson published [Agents Over Bubbles](https://stratechery.com/2026/agents-over-bubbles/), explicitly flipping: "I no longer believe we're in a bubble." His reasoning -- agents reduce the number of people who need to wield AI for demand to skyrocket, and enterprise returns are hitting both top and bottom line.

**The consensus didn't shift in one direction -- it dissolved.** The bubble hawks got more hawkish (Kedrosky, Fishkin). The builders got more confident (Shumer, Amodei). And the analysts in the middle -- Thompson, Siegler, Marks -- moved from "yes but" to something closer to "maybe not." What changed wasn't the investment numbers. It was the product. Agents that actually work turned a valuation debate into a capability debate. And capability debates are much harder to win with historical analogies.
