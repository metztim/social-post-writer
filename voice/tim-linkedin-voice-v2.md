## Voice & Tone

### Pattern Description

Tim's voice is **conversational-intellectual**: he writes like he's talking to a smart colleague at a coffee shop, not presenting at a conference. The tone shifts between:
- Self-deprecating humor ("Deadly sin, I know")
- Genuine curiosity ("My question for today...")
- Technical precision (when describing workflows)
- Reflective depth (when exploring ideas)
- **Playful irony** (when introducing negative concepts: "congrats, you've been workslopped!")

He's never:
- Salesy or promotional
- Overly formal or academic
- Preachy or prescriptive
- Defensive or aggressive
- **Accusatory or blame-focused** (avoids "You did X, I suffer Y" framing)

### Examples from Posts

**Self-deprecating openness:**
> "The reason for trying again is a bit shameful: our Animalz style guide was outdated, and I'd sometimes finish articles so close to deadline that I couldn't run them past our copy-editors anymore."

**Conversational directness:**
> "You think you're designing an AI workflow. Turns out you're peeling an onion with three layers"

**Reflective contemplation:**
> "I've always been agnostic about it: show me a better way to think than writing, and I can be convinced."

**Understated excitement:**
> "It actually worked, and I got back a copy-edited version of my article!"

**Playful irony (NEW):**
> "If this sounds familiar, congrats, you've been workslopped!"

### ‚úÖ DO / ‚ùå DON'T

‚úÖ **DO:**
- Admit mistakes and limitations openly
- Use "I" statements about personal experience
- Write like you're explaining something interesting you discovered
- Mix technical precision with human vulnerability
- Use humor that's self-aware, not self-aggrandizing
- **Add playful irony when introducing negative concepts reader might relate to**
- **Trust the reader to understand implications without editorial commentary**

‚ùå **DON'T:**
- Start with "In today's fast-paced world..."
- Use corporate buzzword soup without irony
- Present yourself as having all the answers
- Hide the messy reality behind polished narratives
- Use excessive enthusiasm or hype
- **Use "colleague" (say "coworker" instead)**
- **Use accusatory "you/I" framing ("You used AI to save time, I lost time")**
- **Add meta-commentary ("There's a name for this now", "Here's what's interesting")**
- **Include editorial flourishes that over-explain what's already clear**

## Structure

### Pattern Description

Tim uses **scaffolding structures** that guide readers through complexity:

1. **Concrete hook** (personal story, specific claim)
2. **Context/backstory** (often admitting a problem or tension)
3. **The thing itself** (numbered steps, examples, or exploration)
4. **Reflection/implication** (what this means more broadly)
5. **Question or invitation** (drawing readers in)

He frequently uses:
- **Numbered lists** for multi-part processes or ideas
- **Bullet points** for parallel items or options (especially stats)
- **Extended metaphors** (onions, layers) sustained throughout
- **Before/after contrasts** (Normal AI vs. Socratic AI)
- **Explicit contradictions** ("Two things I learned contradict each other completely")
- **Line breaks for rhythm** ‚Äî Staccato sentences on separate lines for emotional buildup

### Examples from Posts

**Classic structure (Claude Code style guide):**
1. Hook: "Ever since ChatGPT came out, I've been trying to create an AI styleguide checker."
2. Backstory: "The reason for trying again is a bit shameful..."
3. The process: Three numbered steps for what Claude did
4. The result: "It actually worked..."
5. Invitation: "You can replicate this yourself..."

**Contradiction structure (Superpath daily posting):**
1. Set up tension: "Two things I learned contradict each other completely"
2. Present contradiction 1: "Posting daily is easier..."
3. Present contradiction 2: "Posting daily is too much"
4. Resolve tension: "For October, I'm trying this..."
5. Invite discussion: "If you've done daily posting, what did you learn?"

**Staccato rhythm for emotional buildup (NEW - from workslop post):**
```
But as you start reading, something feels off.

The content is vague.
Key details are missing.
You're not sure what you're supposed to do with this.
```

Each problem on its own line creates mounting tension.

### ‚úÖ DO / ‚ùå DON'T

‚úÖ **DO:**
- Start with the specific, concrete, personal
- Use numbered lists when ideas have sequence or priority
- Create breathing room with line breaks between sections
- Return to opening metaphors or themes at the end
- Give readers a structural roadmap early (numbered list, stated contradiction)
- **Use line breaks to create staccato rhythm when building emotion or listing problems**
- **Give stats visual space with bullets and white space**
- **Merge opening paragraphs for momentum, then break for the "turn" moment**

‚ùå **DON'T:**
- Start with broad abstract generalizations
- Use bullets when sequence matters (use numbers)
- Create walls of text without paragraph breaks
- Introduce new metaphors at the end
- Make readers guess where you're going
- **Cram stats into dense paragraphs**

## Sentence Patterns

### Pattern Description

Tim's sentences have **extreme dynamic range**:
- Long, exploratory sentences (20-35 words) with multiple clauses
- Medium explanatory sentences (10-20 words)
- Short declarative punches (2-6 words) ‚Äî "That's it." "Hard enough."
- **Staccato single-line sentences for emotional emphasis** (NEW)

**Rhythm pattern:** He often follows a long sentence with a short one for emphasis. Also uses line breaks to isolate short sentences for mounting tension.

**Sentence types:**
- Declarative statements (most common)
- Questions (strategic, at transitions and endings)
- Fragments (rare, but used for emphasis)

### ‚úÖ DO / ‚ùå DON'T

‚úÖ **DO:**
- Vary sentence length dramatically within paragraphs
- Use short sentences after complex ideas for emphasis
- Build rhythm with parallel structures in lists
- Let sentences run when exploring complexity
- Use fragments sparingly for punch
- **Use line breaks to isolate short sentences for emotional buildup**

‚ùå **DON'T:**
- Keep all sentences the same length
- Make every sentence complex and long
- Use fragments frequently (loses impact)

## Transitions

### Pattern Description

Tim's transitions are **explicit signposts** that tell you where you're going:

**Transition types:**
1. **Numbered sequences** ("1. First layer... 2. Second layer... 3. Third layer...")
2. **Explicit pivots** ("So what to do with this tension?")
3. **Causal connectors** ("That brought us to step 2...")
4. **Temporal markers** ("Now we've started work on the third layer...")
5. **Logical progressions** ("Of course, all of this is on my mind because...")
6. **Conversational markers (NEW):** "That's not all though."

**Style:**
- Transitions are visible, not hidden
- Often set up as questions before answering
- Use words like: "So," "Now," "Of course," "Example:"
- New sections often get line break + explicit reframe
- **Never use formal transitions:** "Furthermore," "Moreover," "Additionally," "Nevertheless"

### Examples from Posts

**Explicit question transition:**
> "So what to do with this tension?"
> "So yes, there's a need for content created just for AI agents..."

**Numbered progression:**
> "1. Nailing the workflow itself... 2. The 'UX' of the workflow... 3. The organizational 'fallout'..."

**Causal connector:**
> "That brought us to step 2: 'polishing' the workflow..."
> "Of course, all of this is on my mind because AI promises us we can skip or cut short this process."

**Conversational marker (NEW):**
> "That's not all though. The interpersonal cost might be worse."

### ‚úÖ DO / ‚ùå DON'T

‚úÖ **DO:**
- Use numbered sequences when structure is clear
- Set up transitions as questions
- Use explicit markers ("So," "Now," "Example:")
- Signal major shifts with line breaks
- Make the logical flow visible
- **Use conversational transitions like "That's not all though"**

‚ùå **DON'T:**

- **Use formal academic transitions:** "Furthermore," "Moreover," "Additionally," "Nevertheless," "However"

- **Use "Turns out" as a transition phrase** (delete entirely)

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

**‚ùå Starting abstract, getting specific**
- Wrong: "AI is transforming content creation. Here's an example..."
- Right: "I posted every weekday for a month. Here's what I learned..."

**‚ùå Hiding behind polish**
- Wrong: "We successfully implemented an innovative solution..."
- Right: "It was kinda messy, but it actually worked..."

**‚ùå Generic endings**
- Wrong: "What do you think? Let me know in the comments!"
- Right: "If not through writing, how do you think?"

**‚ùå Burying the lede**
- Wrong: Building up slowly to the interesting part
- Right: "It's now a published plugin. In an Animalz plugin marketplace. That didn't exist 30 minutes ago."

**‚ùå Wall-of-text paragraphs**
- Wrong: Multiple ideas crammed into single paragraphs
- Right: One idea per paragraph, white space for breath

**‚ùå Forgetting to ground abstractions**
- Wrong: "AI workflows require careful consideration of organizational impacts"
- Right: "Building such a system is a technical challenge, an expertise challenge, and perhaps even an org design challenge (do we need new / adjusted roles for any of this?)."

**‚ùå Using "colleague" instead of "coworker" (NEW)**
- Wrong: "You open a document from a colleague."
- Right: "You open a document from a coworker."

**‚ùå Adding meta-commentary (NEW)**
- Wrong: "There's a name for this now: workslop"
- Right: "For the work context, researchers from BetterUp Labs and Stanford have coined this 'workslop'"

**‚ùå Accusatory you/I framing (NEW)**
- Wrong: "You used AI to save yourself time by not thinking. I lose time figuring out what you meant."
- Right: [Delete this entirely ‚Äî focus on shared experience, not blame]

**‚ùå Over-complicating CTAs (NEW)**
- Wrong: "My question for today: have you encountered workslop at work? How did you handle it?"
- Right: "Have you been workslopped recently?"

**‚ùå Cramming stats into paragraphs (NEW)**
- Wrong: "The study found that 40% received workslop in the last month, each incident takes nearly 2 hours to deal with, and for a 10,000-person company that's $9 million/year in lost productivity."
- Right:
  ```
  The researchers surveyed 1,150 full-time employees and found that:

  ‚Ä¢ 40% received workslop in the last month
  ‚Ä¢ Each incident takes nearly 2 hours to deal with
  ‚Ä¢ For a 10,000-person company: $9 million/year in lost productivity
  ```
