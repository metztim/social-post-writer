## PART 1: NEGATIVE ANALYSIS - What Tim NEVER Does

### Phrases Tim Avoids (AI Frequently Uses)

1. **"The real problem is..."** / **"The key is..."**
   - AI loves these declarative setups. Tim doesn't use them.
   - Tim's actual pattern: He states problems directly or frames them as tensions/contradictions

2. **"Let's dive in"** / **"Let's explore"** / **"Let's unpack this"**
   - Zero occurrences across all posts
   - Tim just... starts. No announcement of diving.

3. **"Here's the thing:"** / **"Here's what I mean:"**
   - AI uses these to signal transitions. Tim doesn't.
   - Tim's actual connector: "Here's what happened:" (once, in plugin post) - but context-specific, not generic

4. **"At the end of the day"** / **"The bottom line is"**
   - Never appears. Tim doesn't do summary clichés.

5. **"It's not about X, it's about Y"** construction
   - AI adores this false dichotomy pattern
   - Tim DOES use contrasts, but not this formulaic structure
   - His actual pattern: "Two things I learned contradict each other completely:" (more honest, less rhetorical)

6. **"Isn't it...?"** / **"Don't you...?"** (rhetorical questions to reader)
   - Tim asks genuine questions: "If not through writing, how do you think?"
   - Never uses rhetorical questions as persuasion devices

7. **"In today's world"** / **"In the age of AI"**
   - Zero occurrences. No grand temporal framing.

8. **"There's a name for this now" [NEW - from workslop analysis]**
   - Meta-commentary that Tim systematically deletes
   - Instead: Direct introduction with source attribution

9. **"Turns out [statement]" [NEW - from workslop analysis]**
   - AI transition phrase that Tim avoids
   - Instead: State the finding directly

10. **"My question for today:" [NEW - from workslop analysis]**
    - Question framing that gets deleted
    - Instead: Just ask the question directly

### Sentence Structures Tim Avoids

1. **Generic listicles without setup**
   - When Tim uses lists, there's always narrative tension first
   - Never: "Here are 5 ways to..."
   - Always: Problem → tension → "here's what I learned" → numbered insights

2. **Passive construction overuse**
   - Tim uses active voice overwhelmingly
   - When passive appears, it's natural: "I've always been agnostic about it"
   - Never: "It can be seen that..." / "It should be noted that..."

3. **Hedging language clusters**
   - AI loves: "arguably," "perhaps one could say," "it might be suggested"
   - Tim uses "perhaps" once across all posts, and it's genuine uncertainty, not hedging
   - His style: Direct statements with built-in nuance through em-dashes and parentheticals

4. **Academic distance**
   - Never: "One might consider..." / "Research suggests..."
   - Always: "I'm convinced" / "People who love the pen have long argued"

5. **Redundant setup sentences [NEW - from workslop analysis]**
   - If sentence 2 says what sentence 1 said, sentence 1 gets deleted
   - Example deleted: "Workslop doesn't eliminate work. It shifts it downstream." → Kept only: "Workslop shifts work downstream."

6. **Accusatory you/I framing [NEW - from workslop analysis]**
   - Never: "You did X, I suffer Y"
   - Example deleted: "You used AI to save yourself time by not thinking. I lose time figuring out what you meant."
   - Instead: Focus on shared experience, not blame

### Transition Patterns Tim Avoids

1. **Sequential markers:**
   - Never: "Furthermore," "Moreover," "Additionally," "Subsequently"
   - His actual conjunctions: "So," "And," "But" (simple, conversational)

2. **Formal connectors:**
   - Never: "Therefore," "Thus," "Consequently," "Hence"
   - His actual pattern: Starting new paragraphs with independent thoughts (no explicit connector needed)

3. **Meta-commentary transitions:**
   - Never: "To put it another way," "In other words," "To clarify"
   - If he needs to restate, he just restates it differently

4. **Casual research intros [NEW - from workslop analysis]:**
   - Deleted: "Turns out this is everywhere:"

### Line Break and Rhythm Patterns [NEW - from workslop analysis]

**Opening paragraphs: Merged for momentum**
- Continuous flow until the "turn" moment
- Example: "You open a document from a coworker. Looks professional at first. Formatted nicely, plenty of text. But as you start reading, something feels off."
- No line breaks interrupt the build-up

**Problem listing: Staccato rhythm with line breaks**
- After the turn, each problem gets its own line
- Example:
  ```
  The content is vague.
  Key details are missing.
  You're not sure what you're supposed to do with this.
  ```
- Creates mounting tension and emotional emphasis

**Stats presentation: Visual space**
- Never crammed into paragraphs
- Always bullets with white space
- Example:
  ```
  The researchers surveyed 1,150 full-time employees and found that:

  • 40% received workslop in the last month
  • Each incident takes nearly 2 hours to deal with
  • For a 10,000-person company: $9 million/year in lost productivity
  ```

**Pattern:** Line breaks serve emotional rhythm, not just paragraph separation

### What Conjunctions Does He Favor?

**Ranked by frequency:**

1. **"And"** - Most frequent
   - Additive, momentum-building
   - Often starts sentences (informal, conversational)
   - "And I didn't build any of it" / "And since Claude Code can search the web"

2. **"But"** - Used for genuine contrast
   - Never formulaic "but at the same time" constructions

4. **"Or"** - For alternatives
   - "make him rewrite it, or just call it good enough"

5. **"Yet"** - Very rare
   - Only appears once: not his go-to

**Never uses:**
- "However," (too formal)
- "Nevertheless," (too academic)
- "Nonetheless," (never appears)
- "Furthermore," (too structured)

### How He ACTUALLY Links Ideas Paragraph-to-Paragraph

**Method 1: Thematic echo (most common)**
- Para 1 introduces problem
- Para 2 develops it without explicit "As I mentioned" callback
- Reader follows through conceptual thread, not signposted path

**Method 2: Question bridge**
- Paragraph ends with implicit question
- Next paragraph answers it
- Example: "So what to do with this tension?" [new para] "Let go of the daily rhythm..."

**Method 3: Temporal sequence**
- "By the time we had..." → "That brought us to step 2..." → "Now we've started work on the third..."
- Only when walking through actual chronology

**Method 4: Hard cut (white space as transition)**
- Frequent use of paragraph breaks without connective tissue
- Trusts reader to follow jumps
- "Deadly sin, I know." [new para, totally different topic] "In trying to have a solution..."

**Method 5: Callback to opening**
- Opens with scenario → develops ideas → returns to scenario at end
- Never explicit "As I said at the beginning"
- Just: echo of opening language/framing

**Method 6: Conversational marker [NEW]**
- "That's not all though." — added during editing
- Smooths major topic shifts while maintaining flow

**Exclamation points: Very rare, strategic [NEW]**

## PART 7: EDITING PATTERNS - What Gets Cut vs. What Gets Added [NEW]

### Systematic Deletions

**Category 1: Meta-commentary**
- "There's a name for this now"
- "Here's what's interesting"
- "Let me tell you why this matters"
- **Pattern:** If it tells readers how to think, it gets deleted

**Category 2: Redundant setup**
- First sentence that repeats what second sentence says
- Example: "Workslop doesn't eliminate work. It shifts it downstream." → Keep only second sentence
- **Pattern:** If sentence 2 clarifies sentence 1, delete sentence 1

**Category 3: Accusatory framing**
- "You used AI to save time, I lost time"
- Any "You did X, I suffer Y" construction
- **Pattern:** Never blame the reader; focus on shared experience

**Category 4: Editorial flourishes**
- "AI can be powerful for collaboration. But this isn't collaboration—it's cognitive burden-shifting."
- Over-explanation of what's already clear from context
- **Pattern:** Trust the reader to understand implications

**Category 5: Question framing**
- "My question for today:"
- "I want to ask you:"
- **Pattern:** Just ask the question directly

**Category 6: Casual research intros**
- "Turns out this is everywhere:"
- "Turns out [statement]"
- **Pattern:** State findings professionally with specific sources

### Systematic Additions

**Category 1: Playful irony**
- "congrats, you've been workslopped!"
- Added with exclamation point
- **Pattern:** When introducing relatable negative concept

**Category 2: Source specificity**
- Changes: "Stanford surveyed" → "researchers from BetterUp Labs and Stanford"
- Changes: "At work, researchers call it" → "For the work context, researchers from BetterUp Labs and Stanford have coined this"
- **Pattern:** Always include specific organization names

**Category 3: Conversational transitions**
- Adds: "That's not all though."
- Before major topic shifts
- **Pattern:** Smooth transitions while maintaining conversational flow

**Category 4: Source credibility signals**
- Adds: "(Full HBR article in the comments.)"
- At end of post
- **Pattern:** Always reference full source

**Category 5: Line break rhythm**
- Changes dense paragraph to staccato format
- Each problem/symptom gets own line
- **Pattern:** Use line breaks for emotional buildup

**Category 6: Visual formatting for stats**
- Changes paragraph format to bullet format with white space
- **Pattern:** Stats should be scannable, not buried

### Tense Precision Patterns [NEW]

**Past perfect for failed actions:**
- Not: "From the person who should be thinking"
- Instead: "From the person who should have done the thinking"
- **Pattern:** Use past perfect when describing what should have happened but didn't

### The 80/20 Rule of Editing

**80% of editorial work is deletion:**
- Cut meta-commentary
- Cut redundancy
- Cut accusatory language
- Cut editorial flourishes
- Cut over-explanation

**20% of editorial work is strategic addition:**
- Add playful tone markers
- Add source specificity
- Add conversational transitions
- Add visual formatting
- Add source links
